Semantics vs Syntax

Our first topic in the course: propositional logic.

  • Formalize the notion of “validity”
  • Validity of an argument is based on syntax: the form of the argument
  • Truth is a semantic notion: it’s based on the meaning of the terms.

Example

  • If it is raining, I will bring an umbrella.
  • It is raining.
  • Therefore I will bring an umbrella.

This argument is valid.

Non-example

  • If it is raining, I will bring an umbrella.
  • I will bring an umbrella.
  • Therefore it is raining.

This argument is not valid!

Propositional Logic

To analyze the syntactical form of an argument, we use propositional variables.

In algebra, a variable represents a number. In logic, a propositional variable represents the truth value of a statement: that is, either true (T) or false (F).

Validity

  • P: “It is raining.”
  • Q: “I will bring an umbrella.”

Example 1:

  • If P, then Q.
  • \(P\).
  • Therefore, Q.

Example 2:

  • If P, then Q.
  • \(Q\).
  • Therefore, P.

Difference?

  • Forget the meaning of the variables
  • It’s possible for “If P, then Q” to be true, and “Q” to be true, but “P” to be false!
  • Need to analyze the syntactical form.
  • Need to know the propositional connectives.
  • Hence: truth tables.
    • Shows the truth value (T or F) of an expression, based on the values of the variables.

Truth Tables

  • One row for each possible assignment of truth values to the variables.
  • One variable: two rows (T or F). (HW: How many rows for \(n\) variables?)

Example: \(\lnot P\) (“not P”, or “P is not true”). \[ \begin{array}{c | c} p & \lnot p \\ \hline \text{T} & \text{F} \\ \text{F} & \text{T} \end{array} \]

Example: Validity of double negation

  • not not P
  • Therefore P

Truth table:

\[ \begin{array}{c | c | c} p & \lnot p & \lnot \lnot p \\ \hline \text{T} & \text{F} & \text{T} \\ \text{F} & \text{T} & \text{F} \end{array} \]

  • Validity: whenever premises are true, conclusion is.
  • Where is premise true?

and / or

  • P \(\wedge\) Q: “P and Q are both true”
  • P \(\vee\) Q: “P or Q is true”.
    • Inclusive or: P or Q or both are true.
  • Programming example: 
    if (x >= 1 && x <= 8 ) {
  print("x is a valid row on a chessboard")
}
if (x >= 1 || x <= 8) {
  print("This will always print.")
}

Coda

Are the following two statements logically equivalent for all integers \(x\)?

  • “It is false that \(x\) is both an even number and is greater than 7.”
  • “x is an odd number less than or equal to 7.”

In other words: is “NOT (even AND greater than 7)” equivalent to “not even AND not greater than 7”?

De Morgan’s Laws

Notice:

  • p \(\vee\) q: “at least one of p or q is true”.
  • \(\lnot\) (p \(\wedge\) q): “at least one is false”.

De Morgan’s Laws: (Homework: prove these!)

  1. The truth values of \(\lnot (p \wedge q)\) and \(\lnot p \vee \lnot q\) are the same.
  2. The truth values of \(\lnot (p \vee q)\) and \(\lnot p \wedge \lnot q\) are the same.

Conditionals

  • Material conditional, or implication
  • p \(\rightarrow\) q: “If p, then q”, or “p implies q”.

\[ \begin{array}{c | c | c} p & q & p \rightarrow q \\ \hline \text{T} & \text{T} & \text{T} \\ \text{T} & \text{F} & \text{F} \\ \text{F} & \text{T} & \text{T} \\ \text{F} & \text{F} & \text{T} \end{array} \]

Examples

Example 1:

  • Parent: “If you clean your room I will give you ice cream.”
  • Child: cleans room
  • Parent must give ice cream in order to not be a liar.

Example 2:

  • Parent: “If you get a 100 on your algebra regents exam I will buy you a Playstation 5.”
  • Child: gets a 99.
  • Parent: kinda want to buy a PS5 anyway. (Not lying!)

Mathematical Examples

  • Real motivation for defining conditionals this way:
  • “If \(x > 5\) then \(x > 3\)”.
  • Surely that is true.
  • Technically does not have a truth value
    • it is a formula, not a statement
    • need to plug in for \(x\).
  • x = 4: “If 4 > 5, then 4 > 3”. Is this true?
  • “Every number greater than 5 is also greater than 3.” Clearly true.

Biconditionals

  • p \(\leftrightarrow\) q: “p if and only if q”
  • “p is necessary and sufficient for q”
  • “If p, then q, and if q, then p.”
  • “p and q have the same truth value”.

Truth Table

\[ \begin{array}{c | c | c} p & q & p \leftrightarrow q \\ \hline \text{T} & \text{T} & \text{T} \\ \text{T} & \text{F} & \text{F} \\ \text{F} & \text{T} & \text{F} \\ \text{F} & \text{F} & \text{T} \end{array} \]

Propaganda

  • Rules given today: classical logic.
  • Who defines the rules for truth?
  • There are non-classical logics:
    • denying excluded middle / double negation
    • relevance logics (conditionals)
    • many others
    • Useful for studying different domains.

Constructivism

  • Classical mathematicians: every precisely defined mathematical statement is either true or false.
  • Constructivist mathematics: statements are only true when we can construct a proof of it (“there is an algorithm which proves \(p\)”)
    • only false when a construction can be given that disproves it
    • a “conjecture” (that has not yet been proven)? Neither true nor false

Constructivism / Double Negation

  • \(\lnot p\) is true if you can construct a refutation of \(p\) (if you can disprove \(p\)).
  • \(\lnot \lnot p\) is true if you can construct a refutation of the statement that you cannot disprove \(p\).
  • If you can prove that you cannot disprove \(p\), is that a proof of \(p\)?
    • Classically, yes
    • Constructively, no. It does not give you a construction of an actual proof of \(p\)!
  • Law of Excluded Middle: \(p \vee \lnot p\)?

Suppose \(p\) is the statement “I study for the Discrete Mathematics exam” and \(q\) is the statement “I score a 100% on the Discrete Mathematics exam.” Then, in English, \(p \rightarrow q\) means “If I study for the Discrete Mathematics exam, then I score a 100% on the Discrete Mathematics exam.”

Determine what the following statements represent in English:

  • the converse: \(q \rightarrow p\)
  • the inverse: \(\lnot p \rightarrow \lnot q\)
  • the contrapositive: \(\lnot q \rightarrow \lnot p\)

  1. A tautology is a statement that is always true. Show that \(p \vee \lnot p\) is a tautology (by making a truth table).

Note: the assertion that \(p \vee \lnot p\) is always true is referred to as the “Law of Excluded Middle”. This means that there is no “middle ground” for truth values: every statement is either true or false.
