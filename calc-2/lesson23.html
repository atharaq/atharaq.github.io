Moral

  • There are lots of intuitive arguments regarding limits that don’t hold up.
  • The limit of a sequence of integrals is not necessarily equal to the integral of the limit of the sequence.
  • These kinds of counterintuitive ideas are explored in real analysis (not offered here).
  • This is why it took so long (in history) for limits to be made rigorous!

  • Complex powers?
  • \(e^{i\pi}\)?
  • Use the Taylor series!

Taylor series

\[\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(i\cdot \pi)^n}{n!}\]

  • \(\sum \frac{x^n}{n!}\) converges absolutely for every \(x\), including complex numbers!
  • Can just use the Taylor series then to define \(e\) to imaginary / complex powers!

\[1 + (i \pi) + \frac{i^2 \pi^2}{2} + \frac{i^3 \pi^3}{6} + \ldots\]

Hmm…

Notice that \(i^2 = -1\), \(i^3 = -i\), \(i^4 = 1\), etc. So this simplifies!

\[ 1 + i\pi - \frac{\pi^2}{2} - \frac{i\pi^3}{6} + \frac{\pi^4}{4!} - \ldots \]

  • Kind-of alternating series? Not exactly?
  • Sometimes \(i\) is there, sometimes not?
